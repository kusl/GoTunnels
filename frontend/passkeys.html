Passkeys, in plain English

This whole site is a throwaway sandbox that exists so you can poke at passkeys (and a strict Content-Security-Policy) without risking anything real. Here is what passkeys are, how this site uses them, and some exercises to try on your phone.

What a passkey actually is

A passkey is a pair of mathematically linked keys. The private key never leaves your device (or your platform's encrypted sync, like iCloud Keychain or Google Password Manager). The public key is the only thing this site ever stores. When you sign in, the site sends a fresh random challenge; your device signs it with the private key after you approve with Face ID, a fingerprint, or your device PIN; the site checks the signature against the public key it stored. No shared secret ever crosses the network, so there is nothing to phish, leak, or reuse.

The part that quietly kills phishing: a passkey is bound to the website's identity — its Relying Party ID, essentially the hostname. Your browser simply will not offer a passkey made for one site to a different site, no matter how convincing the lookalike is. A password can be typed into the wrong box; a passkey cannot.

Every ./scripts/up.sh run gets a brand-new random *.trycloudflare.com hostname, and passkeys made here are bound to that hostname. When the stack is torn down, the hostname is gone forever and every passkey bound to it becomes a harmless orphan in your password manager — which is exactly what makes this a safe playground. Delete them whenever you like.

How this site implements them

The backend is Go with the go-webauthn library. A ceremony ("begin") stores a short-lived server-side flow — it expires after 10 minutes — and pins the exact browser origin that started it; the "finish" step must complete against that same origin. Registration asks your authenticator to create a discoverable credential where supported, sign-in verifies the signature and counter, and both paths record an entry you can see on the Activity page. Accounts can be created with a passkey alone — no password ever exists for such an account — or with a password to which you can add passkeys later from Settings.

Try it on your phone

iPhone (iOS 16+): passkeys live in iCloud Keychain. Safari or Chrome will offer Face ID / Touch ID when this site asks. Manage or delete them under Settings → Passwords.

Android (9+): passkeys live in Google Password Manager (or another manager you have set as the provider). Chrome prompts with your fingerprint or screen lock. Manage them in Settings → Passwords & accounts.

Cross-device: on a desktop browser, choose "use a phone or tablet" when prompted — you scan a QR code with the phone that holds the passkey, and the two devices verify proximity over Bluetooth before the phone signs. That proximity check is another anti-phishing property: a remote attacker cannot relay it.

Sandbox exercises

  1. On your phone, open this site's signup page and create an account with Create account with a passkey — pick any username; there is no email step. Notice a password never existed.
  2. Log out, then sign back in from the login page with the passkey button. Watch how the prompt names this exact hostname.
  3. Open your device's password manager and find the passkey filed under this deployment's hostname. This is where you would delete it later.
  4. If you have a laptop nearby: start a passkey login there, pick "use a phone", and scan the QR with the phone that holds the passkey. That is the cross-device flow.
  5. Restart the stack (./scripts/down.sh then ./scripts/up.sh) and note the new hostname: the old passkey is never offered here again, because the site identity changed. That is origin binding doing its job.

Questions people actually ask

Can I sign up with just a passkey — no password at all?
Yes, here you can. The signup page has a passkey-only path: the account is created at the moment your authenticator approves, with a passkey as its only credential. Password login for such an account simply fails with the same generic error as any wrong password, so its existence leaks nothing.
What if I lose the device holding my passkey?
On real accounts you would rely on platform sync (iCloud/Google) or a second registered credential. Here, losing it costs you a demo account on a hostname that disappears at the next restart — that is the point of the sandbox.
Is my fingerprint or face sent to the site?
Never. Biometrics only unlock the private key locally; the site sees a signature, nothing biometric.
Why does the passkey prompt sometimes not appear?
Usually one of: the browser lacks support (see the panel above), the page is not on HTTPS, or the passkey you are trying to use was created for a different hostname — including this site's previous tunnel hostname.
